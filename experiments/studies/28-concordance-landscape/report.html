Concordance coefficients in software and the literature: where misskappa sits

Question

misskappa is a missing-data agreement package: it estimates the Cohen / Conger / Fleiss / Brennan–Prediger kappa family and coefficient alpha under MCAR (inverse-probability weighting) and MAR (FIML), with influence-function standard errors and Wald equality tests. It is not advertised as a concordance-correlation-coefficient (CCC) tool. Yet its quadratically weighted Conger coefficient is Lin’s CCC for two raters on continuous data (van Oest & Moss 2026), and its worked dataset — the Vanbelle (2019) CRACKLES study — is exactly the repeated-measurement agreement problem the biostatistics CCC literature was built for.

So: where does misskappa sit in the concordance-coefficient software and literature landscape? Which packages own the territory it does not, how do the CCC people handle repeated measurement, and what is the “unbiased CCC” that neither misskappa nor the plain Lin estimator computes?

Short answer

misskappa and the CCC world are complementary, not competing. The CCC tradition (Lin 1989; King & Chinchilli 2001; Carrasco & Jover 2003) is a continuous, complete-data, mixed-model / U-statistic enterprise (cccrm, SimplyAgree). misskappa owns the orthogonal axis it was built for — missing data (IPW, FIML) and categorical / vector-valued agreement — but the overlap is larger than it looks: its quadratic Conger kappa reproduces Lin’s CCC to machine precision (confirmed against DescTools, cccrm::cccUst, and the moment plug-in at 0.915173), and — the new finding in this report — its vector path reproduces the King–Chinchilli–Carrasco repeated-measures U-statistic CCC exactly (its Dmat is misskappa’s component-weight matrix \(W\)). So the two are the same estimator across the continuous-agreement range; what the CCC packages still own alone is the mixed-model / longitudinal route (ccc_vc, time trends, covariates), and what misskappa owns alone is missing data and influence-function inference.

The “unbiased CCC” question has a sharper answer than the first draft of this report gave. Carrasco’s variance-components estimator corrects one thing — the squared mean-difference term in the denominator — and that correction does almost nothing (it moves the bpres CCC by \(2.0e-04\), and is sub-percent for any usable \(n\)). More importantly, a denominator patch is not a proper bias correction at all: it removes only \(21\%\) of the moment estimator’s actual bias. The genuine \(O(1/n)\) bias of a CCC — a ratio of moments — is the nonlinearity bias \(\mathbb E[\hat N/\hat D]\neq \mathbb E[\hat N]/\mathbb E[\hat D]\), which a proper second-order correction (the second-order influence function / von Mises expansion, or equivalently a jackknife) removes: \(96\%\) of the bias, versus the denominator patch’s sliver. Since misskappa already exposes per-subject (first-order) influence functions, the principled addition is that second-order correction, not a denominator tweak — and even it only matters at small \(n\).

The table splits cleanly. Continuous concordance is dominated by cccrm (Carrasco’s package: variance-components ccc_vc, U-statistic cccUst, longitudinal ccclon) and SimplyAgree (Caldwell’s modern agreement toolkit, popular in sports and clinical science), with epiR, DescTools, and pingouin/Stata/SAS offering the plain paired Lin CCC. Method-comparison lives in MethComp (Carstensen — Deming regression, limits of agreement) and blandr. Categorical agreement is irr, irrCAC (Gwet’s chance-corrected coefficients), vcd, and psych, with multiagree (Vanbelle) the one tool that handles multilevel / clustered multirater kappa — and the source of our CRACKLES data. Across all of them, none estimates agreement under missing data with a principled MCAR/MAR model; that column is misskappa’s alone. Conversely, none of misskappa’s estimators target longitudinal CCC.

The CCC \(\leftrightarrow\) misskappa bridge

Lin’s concordance correlation coefficient for two methods \(X,Y\) is \[ \rho_c \;=\; \frac{2\,\sigma_{xy}}{\sigma_x^2+\sigma_y^2+(\mu_x-\mu_y)^2}, \] the Pearson correlation shrunk by a bias-correction factor for location and scale shift. The quadratically weighted two-rater Conger coefficient is the same ratio of disagreement to chance-disagreement, so the two coincide (van Oest & Moss 2026). The benchmark confirms it: on the systolic blood-pressure data (cccrm::bpres, first replicate, \(n=384\)),

Lin’s CCC on bpres (n = 384): four implementations agree; ccc_vc is the bias-corrected outlier.
implementation CCC note
misskappa (quadratic Conger kappa) 0.9151726 pairwise-available moment estimator
moment plug-in (1/n), by hand 0.9151726 Lin (1989) definition
DescTools::CCC 0.9151726 Lin’s CCC, z-transform CI
cccrm::cccUst (U-statistic) 0.9151726 King-Chinchilli-Carrasco U-statistic
cccrm::ccc_vc (variance components) 0.9153748 Carrasco & Jover REML – BIAS-CORRECTED

The first four rows are identical to seven decimals (0.9151726): misskappa’s quadratic Conger kappa, the hand-coded moment plug-in, DescTools::CCC, and cccrm’s U-statistic cccUst all compute the same Lin coefficient. The fifth, Carrasco’s variance-components ccc_vc, sits slightly higher (0.9153748) — the bias correction discussed below. The equivalence is not an artefact of this dataset: it also holds on irr::anxiety, where the two raters happen to share a mean so the location term vanishes entirely.

How the CCC literature handles repeated measurement

The interesting question is the one the Vanbelle data poses: what happens when each subject is measured more than once per method — replicates, time points, or clustered sites? The literature offers three paradigms, and the benchmark runs all three on the full bpres data (two devices, two replicates per subject):

Repeated-measures CCC on bpres (2 methods x 2 replicates).
implementation paradigm CCC SE 95% CI
cccrm::ccc_vc variance components (REML) 0.9150 0.0070 (0.900, 0.928)
cccrm::cccUst U-statistics 0.9148 0.0103 (0.892, 0.933)
SimplyAgree::agree_reps (ccc) U-statistics (K-C-C) 0.9157 – (0.894, 0.933)
misskappa (vector path) U-statistics (replicates as vector) 0.9148 0.0195 (0.877, 0.953)

  1. Variance components / linear mixed model (Carrasco & Jover 2003; cccrm::ccc_vc). Fit a mixed model with subject, method, and (optionally) time random/fixed effects by REML, then form the CCC as a ratio of variance components — structurally an intraclass-correlation argument. This is the route that generalizes to longitudinal data, covariates, and non-Gaussian outcomes (Carrasco 2010 extends it to overdispersed counts). Here it gives 0.9150.

  2. U-statistics (King & Chinchilli 2001; King, Chinchilli & Carrasco 2007; cccrm::cccUst, and SimplyAgree). Build the CCC from \(U\)-statistics of pairwise within- and between-subject squared differences, time-weighting the replicate structure. It is distribution-free; here 0.9148 (cccrm) and 0.9157 (SimplyAgree).

  3. GEE / modelling (Barnhart & Williamson 2001; Barnhart, Haber & Song 2002 for the overall CCC across many observers; King & Chinchilli 2001 for categorical data). Estimating equations on transformed agreement contrasts, useful when covariate adjustment is the point.

misskappa has no mixed-model CCC — but it is not absent from the table. Its vector path handles the replicate structure directly: treat the two methods as raters and the two replicates as components of a rating vector. That reproduces the U-statistic value exactly (0.914794 vs cccUst’s 0.914794), which is not a coincidence — it is the subject of the next section.

The U-statistic estimator is misskappa’s vector path

The U-statistic route (King–Chinchilli–Carrasco; cccUst) is the closest prior art to misskappa’s quadratic / vector machinery, and the relationship is exact, not analogical. Two facts pin it down.

One estimator dials from kappa to CCC. cccUst carries a delta argument: the power of the per-component distance. delta = 0 gives a 0/1 distance, delta = 1 a squared distance, so a single estimator spans the categorical and continuous ends of agreement:

cccUst’s delta dial: one estimator from unweighted kappa (delta=0) to Lin’s CCC (delta=1), matched by misskappa and the standard packages at both ends.
delta distance target cccUst misskappa cross-check
0 0/1 (nominal) unweighted kappa 0.32506 0.32506 0.32506 (irr::kappa2)
1 squared Lin CCC = quadratic kappa 0.69820 0.69820 0.69820 (DescTools::CCC)

At both ends it matches to the shown digits: delta = 0 reproduces the unweighted Cohen kappa, delta = 1 reproduces Lin’s CCC = the quadratically weighted kappa. This is the kappa = CCC identity (van Oest & Moss 2026), built into software from the CCC side ever since King & Chinchilli (2001).

The repeated-measures kernel is the vector loss. cccUst represents each subject as a profile over occasions and compares two profiles with a quadratic form \(v^\top \mathbf D\, v\), \(v = |X-Y|^{\delta}\), where \(\mathbf D\) (the Dmat argument) weights the occasions; the coefficient is again \(1 - \text{within} / \text{between}\), with the subject as the i.i.d. unit and the per-subject \(U\)-statistic projection serving as the influence function. Put \(\mathbf D = W\) and “occasion” = “vector component” and this is exactly misskappa’s component-separable quadratic kappa:

cccUst(Dmat) vs misskappa vector kappa (W = Dmat): identical on the diagonal, divergent off it.
weights cccUst misskappa vector difference
diagonal weights (W diagonal) 0.805652 0.805652 -1.1e-16
off-diagonal weights (W[1,2]=1.5) 0.787802 0.843241 -5.5e-02

With diagonal weights the two are identical to machine precision (\(-1.1e-16\)) — which is why misskappa’s vector path reproduced cccUst on bpres exactly. They diverge only off the diagonal, by a real and principled amount (\(-0.055\) here): misskappa uses the signed quadratic form \((x-y)^\top W (x-y) = \lVert L(x-y)\rVert^2\) — a genuine weighted-Euclidean (Mahalanobis) distance — whereas cccUst uses absolute cross-terms \(\sum_{st} D_{st}\,|x_s-y_s|\,|x_t-y_t|\), which is not induced by any metric on the rating space and discards the sign of joint errors. The two coincide exactly when \(W\) is diagonal (signs are then irrelevant); off-diagonal, misskappa’s is the correct generalization. Off-diagonal weights are essentially never used in the CCC literature, but they are meaningful here — misskappa’s CRACKLES analysis couples left/right of each thoracic site — so this is the regime where the distinction bites.

So misskappa’s vector kappa and the King–Chinchilli–Carrasco repeated-measures CCC are the same construction from opposite ends. The honest positioning: KCC own the unified, distribution-free, repeated-measures CCC estimator; misskappa adds the missing-data layer (IPW / FIML — cccUst is listwise only), influence-function inference with Wald / joint equality tests, the kappa = CCC identity (they had it as an estimator, not a theorem), and the extension to more than two raters and to categorical data.

The “unbiased CCC” question

misskappa (like the plain Lin estimator) computes the moment plug-in \(\hat\rho_c=\hat N/\hat D\) with \(\hat N=2s_{xy}\) and \(\hat D=s_x^2+s_y^2+(\bar x-\bar y)^2\), which is biased in finite samples. Two things are often conflated here, and neither is what a real correction needs.

It is not the \(/n\) vs \(/(n-1)\) choice. In the ratio the \(1/n\) factor cancels between the numerator and the variance terms of the denominator, so switching to unbiased second moments changes \(\hat\rho_c\) only through the unscaled location term — a sub-\(10^{-4}\) effect.

It is not the location-term “correction” either. One can note that \(\mathbb E[(\bar x-\bar y)^2]=(\mu_x-\mu_y)^2+\operatorname{Var}(\bar x-\bar y)\) and subtract \(\operatorname{Var}(\bar x-\bar y)=(\sigma_x^2+\sigma_y^2-2\sigma_{xy})/n\) from the denominator — which is roughly what Carrasco & Jover’s variance-components estimator does implicitly (a truncated between-method variance component). But this patches one term of one moment and ignores the dominant bias. In fact the \(1/n\) biases of \(\hat N\) and \(\hat D\) are both \(-2\sigma_{xy}/n\) and largely cancel in the ratio; the leading \(O(1/n)\) bias is the ratio nonlinearity \(\mathbb E[\hat N/\hat D]\neq\mathbb E[\hat N]/\mathbb E[\hat D]\) — the Jacobian-curvature term that the delta method drops at first order. The location patch does not touch it.

The benchmark makes the gap concrete. Against a known true CCC, we compare the moment estimator, the location patch, and a jackknife — which is a proper nonparametric estimator of the \(O(1/n)\) bias (the same quantity the second-order influence function / von Mises expansion targets):

The denominator patch removes only \(13\)–\(31\%\) of the moment bias; the jackknife removes \(61\)–\(118\%\) (values near or above 100% are Monte-Carlo scatter on a residual bias already at the noise floor). So the resolution of the user’s question is two-layered: Carrasco’s variance-components route does basically nothing to the point estimate on non-repeated data — \(2.0e-04\) on bpres, and sub-percent for any usable \(n\) (it earns its keep on repeated structure, not on bias) — and the denominator correction it embodies is not a proper bias correction anyway. A genuine unbiased-CCC for misskappa would be a second-order influence-function (or jackknife / bootstrap) correction of the whole ratio functional. That is natural here precisely because every misskappa estimator already exposes its per-subject first-order influence functions — the second-order term is the missing piece — though, as the table shows, it only matters below \(n\approx 30\).

Categorical and vector-valued agreement (CRACKLES)

On the other side of misskappa’s range — categorical and vector-valued data — the comparators are irr and irrCAC, and here misskappa reproduces them and then extends past them. Using the four expert observers (EXP1–EXP4) on the CRACKLES data:

At a single chest site (a scalar binary rating), misskappa’s Fleiss kappa matches irr::kappam.fleiss and irrCAC::fleiss.kappa.raw exactly (0.6354), and its Conger kappa matches irrCAC::conger.kappa.raw (0.6364) — and the IPW and Cat-FIML estimators agree on the complete data, as they must. The pay-off is the last block: pooling all six sites into a vector-valued rating with a Hamming component loss gives a single six-site agreement coefficient (Conger 0.5192, Fleiss 0.5150) that none of irr, irrCAC, or DescTools can produce. This is misskappa’s own answer to “repeated measurement”: treat the repeats as components of a vector, not as levels of a random effect.

Vanbelle’s multilevel kappa vs the vector path

CRACKLES has been analysed before — by Vanbelle (2019), whose multilevel multirater kappa is the standard treatment of sites within patients. The two coefficients take opposite routes through the same repeated structure, and it is worth seeing exactly how they relate. Vanbelle collapses the six sites: she pools the site-level ratings and chance-corrects with the pooled marginals, under a within-cluster homogeneity assumption — that every site of a patient shares the same classification probabilities — yielding a scalar kappa with a patient-clustered SE. misskappa keeps the six sites as a vector and chance-corrects each site with its own marginals.

On the real data they differ, and the difference is informative. The pooled coefficient reproduces Vanbelle’s published Table 3 values to the decimal (a check that pooling is her point estimate); the vector coefficient runs systematically lower:

The gap is not noise — it is exactly the between-site covariance of the raters’ marginals in the chance term, \[ P_e^{\text{misskappa}}-P_e^{\text{Vanbelle}} = \sum_j \operatorname{Cov}_s\!\big(p_j(r_1,s),\,p_j(r_2,s)\big), \] which vanishes precisely when the sites are identically distributed. So the two coincide iff homogeneity holds, which a synthetic check confirms:

Homogeneity is the fork: identical site marginals -> the two agree; differing marginals -> they split.
scenario misskappa vector Vanbelle pooled difference
homogeneous (all 6 sites p=0.30) 0.3749 0.3755 -0.0006
heterogeneous (sites differ) 0.3486 0.3954 -0.0468

With identical site marginals the two agree (residual -0.0006 is Monte-Carlo noise); with differing marginals they split by 0.047, the same kind of margin as the real data. On CRACKLES the sites are very different (anterior crackles are near-absent), so misskappa’s per-site chance correction discounts the easy “no crackle anywhere” agreement that Vanbelle’s pooled correction partly credits — hence the lower, more conservative coefficient. Neither is wrong; they chance-correct at different levels, and homogeneity is the exact condition under which the level stops mattering. Vanbelle’s own Table 2 rejects that assumption at \(p<0.0001\) (and she computes the pooled coefficient anyway, for sample-size reasons) — so on this dataset the two coefficients are genuinely estimating different things.

On the inference side the two agree more than the point estimates suggest: misskappa’s vector SE already treats the patient as the i.i.d. unit (the rating vector is the patient’s observation), so it clusters at the patient level by construction — the same level Vanbelle’s multilevel variance targets. The pooled stack above is used only to recover her point estimate; its naive one-row-per-site SE would understate the uncertainty, which is exactly the clustering problem her paper exists to fix.

Where this leaves misskappa

  • Owns: missing-data agreement (IPW under MCAR, FIML under MAR) for the categorical kappa family and alpha; vector-valued / component-separable agreement; a quadratic coefficient that is Lin’s CCC for two continuous raters — and, via the vector path, the King–Chinchilli–Carrasco repeated-measures U-statistic CCC (reproduced exactly) — all with influence-function SEs and Wald equality tests.
  • Does not own (CCC world owns): the mixed-model / longitudinal CCC route (cccrm::ccc_vc: time trends, covariates, non-Gaussian outcomes); the overall CCC across many observers (Barnhart 2002); method-comparison tooling — Bland–Altman, Deming (MethComp, blandr); multilevel clustered kappa (multiagree).
  • Could add (but low priority): a proper second-order bias correction for the pairwise quadratic / CCC estimator — a second-order influence-function (or jackknife / bootstrap) correction of the ratio functional, not the denominator patch, which removes only a sliver of the bias. misskappa already exposes first-order influence functions, so the machinery is half built; but the effect is sub-percent above \(n\approx 30\), so this is a nicety, not a fix.

Caveats

  • The equivalence and bias results use complete continuous data; the point of misskappa is the missing-data case, where the CCC packages (all listwise-only) have no comparator. A genuine missing-data CCC benchmark would require simulating MCAR/MAR amputation and has no external baseline — it belongs in the FIML/quadratic simulation experiments, not here.
  • epiR::epi.ccc (a fourth Lin implementation) could not be installed on this box: epiR pulls sf/GDAL system libraries that are absent. DescTools, cccrm, and the hand-coded plug-in already give three independent Lin implementations, so the equivalence is not under-witnessed.
  • The repeated-measures CCCs from the three paradigms differ in the third decimal (0.915–0.916); this is expected (different estimands and small-sample behaviour), not an error. SimplyAgree::agree_reps is formally deprecated in favour of agreement_limit but still computes the K-C-C CCC.
  • multiagree is the right tool when the CRACKLES clustering (sites within patients) is modelled as such; experiment 25 already reproduces Vanbelle’s weighted kappas exactly against it. Here we use the EXP subgroup descriptively.
  • The bias results use a Gaussian DGP and the jackknife as the reference “proper” corrector; deriving the second-order influence function of the weighted multirater CCC in closed form (so a one-pass, no-resampling correction with a consistent SE is available) is the actual work a future “unbiased misskappa” would need.
